Writing Sample

YOUR ASK
You asked about the Temporary Reauthorization and Study of the Emergency Scheduling of Fentanyl Analogues Act’s (S. 3201) impact on criminal justice, specifically how it would impact mandatory minimum sentencing. You also asked about support and opposition from Members of the Congressional Black Caucus.
BACKGROUND
In 2018, the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) issued a temporary order to address the opioid crisis in the US. The order placed 17 fentanyl-related substances (fentanyl analogues) as Schedule I drugs, meaning the drugs have no medical use and have a high potential for abuse. Schedule I categorizations carry the most severe criminal penalties. The two-year order expires on February 6, 2020 and cannot be extended without congressional action.
S. 3201, which is being voted on under suspension today, will extend the DEA’s temporary order for an additional 15 months and authorize a GAO study on fentanyl drugs. While the DOJ and law enforcement claim the order allows them to prevent fentanyl-related overdoses, criminal justice organizations, such as the ACLU, cite that 77% of individuals prosecuted for fentanyl-related substances were people of color, 39.1% of whom were Black. Of those, 97.4% were sentenced to prison, and 44.6% carried a mandatory minimum penalty (some could seek relief). If S. 3201 passes, it will:
  • Continue to allow for simple possession as grounds for prosecution (instead of a case-by case determination)
  • Extend mandatory minimum sentencing provisions
  • Continue to tie mandatory minimum sentencing to the quantity of fentanyl in possession
If it does not pass, the order will expire next week. Fentanyl will remain illegal if S. 3201 does not pass, and prosecutors will need to use the Federal Analogue Act to prosecute fentanyl-related crimes on a case-by-case determination. Under the Federal Analogue Act, mandatory minimums can only be invoked with death or serious bodily injury (not quantity or possession).
SUPPORT AND OPPOSITION
The bill passed the Senate unanimously, but ran into issues yesterday at a Crime, Terrorism, and Homeland Security subcommittee hearing:
  • Chairwoman Karen Bass said she is concerned that federal sentencing guidelines related to fentanyl may mean many defendants who are not high-level traffickers may be unnecessarily subjected to mandatory minimums that in fact become life sentences. She said that the DOJ can still prosecute individuals under a related piece of legislation, the Federal Analogue Act.
  • Congresswoman Lucy McBath asked about the seemingly conflicting goals of public health and criminal justice equity.
  • Congresswoman Lee believes that fentanyl should remain Schedule I, though voiced opposition to mandatory minimum sentencing.
  • The Federal Public Defender in the Northern District of Alabama is in opposition, and said:
    • “There are troubling signs that Congress’s response to fentanyl threatens to erase the gains of the past decade by returning to the failed and unjust strategies of the drug war. Some legislators who have supported the bipartisan movement away from the War on Drugs are nevertheless endorsing a harsh and punitive response to fentanyl.”
Groups in favor of the bill generally call for the one-year extension in order to conduct studies, produce a report, and prepare a more substantive response in the future. However, the ACLU and 46 other organizations opposed the bill in a recent coalition letter. Some conservative groups, such as FreedomWorks, a libertarian group, also oppose the bill. The top five districts for fentanyl trafficking offenders – located in Massachusetts, Ohio, California, and New Hampshire – have several CBC members as well. However, the bill has support from all 50 attorneys general.
